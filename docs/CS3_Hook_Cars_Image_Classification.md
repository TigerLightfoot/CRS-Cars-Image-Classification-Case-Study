# Can Computer Vision Recognize Car Models?

## Case Study Mission: Fine-Grained Vehicle Image Classification

You have just been hired by a small transportation analytics company that wants to explore whether computer vision can identify car models from images. The company is not asking you to build a perfect production system. Instead, they want a first-pass case study that answers a more realistic question:

**Can a machine learning model classify car models from images, and what makes this task difficult?**

## Your Role

You are the data scientist in charge of the first analysis. You will work with the CRS Cars Dataset, a vehicle image dataset based on the Stanford Cars Dataset and prepared by Meta-Album for image classification and benchmark research. Your goal is to turn a large, complicated visual recognition problem into a clear, reproducible machine learning workflow.

## Why This Matters

Car classification is harder than it looks. Many car models have similar shapes, colors, and body styles. A model may be able to separate very different vehicles, but struggle with fine-grained differences between similar makes and models. This makes the project a strong example of how machine learning performance depends not only on the algorithm, but also on the structure of the data and the difficulty of the labels.

## The Challenge

Using the CRS Cars Dataset, build and evaluate an image classification model. You may use the Micro version of the dataset for a smaller project or a larger version if your computer can handle it. Your model should classify car images into their correct categories and your report should explain how well it worked.

## Your Final Deliverable

You will produce a short technical case study and a GitHub repository. Your report should include:

- the real-world motivation,
- a description of the dataset,
- your preprocessing steps,
- your model approach,
- your evaluation results,
- and a discussion of limitations.

## Remember

The goal is not to prove that AI can identify every car perfectly. The goal is to practice building a responsible, reproducible image classification project and to explain what your results actually mean.
